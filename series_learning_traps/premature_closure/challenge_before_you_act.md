# Challenge Before You Act

Use this immediately after reaching a conclusion — before committing to it. The prompt keeps the question open for one more round, surfaces the most fragile assumptions, forces the opposite case, and names what the current line of thinking has most likely skipped.

The risk it addresses: cognitive closure. The satisfying sense of having resolved a problem arrives before the depth has been found. This prompt builds in a deliberate pause between conclusion and action.

---

## The prompt

```
I've just reached a conclusion about [SITUATION/PROBLEM]. Here's how I reasoned my way to it: [BRIEF SUMMARY OF YOUR THINKING — 3-5 sentences].

Challenge this before I act. Which assumptions is this conclusion most dependent on — and which of those are most fragile? What would someone who reached the opposite conclusion say using the same information? What has this line of thinking most likely skipped over or ignored?

End with three specific questions I should be able to answer before I commit to this direction.
```

---

**How to use:** Run this after you feel resolved, not before. The prompt is designed to catch closure that has already happened — not to help you think through an open problem. Write the reasoning summary honestly; the less curated it is, the more useful the challenge will be.

**What this reveals:** The assumptions the conclusion is leaning on hardest, and whether those assumptions are load-bearing or just convenient. If the three closing questions feel answerable immediately, the conclusion is probably sound. If they reveal things you haven't considered, stay one more round.
