# Build Transfer Through Varied Practice

Use this to build genuine fluency in a skill across many contexts, rather than repeated practice in the same conditions. The prompt generates five meaningfully different scenarios and debriefs after each attempt on what carried over and what collapsed when the context changed.

The risk it addresses: single-context encoding. A skill practised only in the conditions where it was learned is encoded with those conditions. Transfer — the ability to apply it in genuinely different situations — requires exposure to varied contexts, not just more repetitions of the same one.

---

## The prompt

```
I want to build real fluency in [SKILL] across different contexts, not just in the conditions where I learned it.

Give me five meaningfully different scenarios that all require this skill but vary in: stakes, relationship type, emotional complexity, and available information. Let me work through each one. After each attempt, tell me what carried over well and what collapsed when the context changed.
```

---

**How to use:** Work through all five scenarios, even the ones that feel outside your comfort zone. The variance is the point — the scenarios where the skill feels least applicable are the ones doing the most work. Don't skim past the debrief after each one; the pattern across five attempts is more informative than any single result.

**What this reveals:** The conditions under which the skill is robust vs. the conditions where it breaks down. After five varied scenarios you'll have a specific map of where the skill holds and where it collapses — which is exactly what transfer training is meant to produce.
