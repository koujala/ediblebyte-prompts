# Board Narrative Draft

Use this to translate financial results into a clear, decision-focused narrative for a board, executive, or investor audience. You provide the numbers and the context — the model drafts the narrative. Your financial expertise validates every number, claim, and conclusion before it goes anywhere.

---

```
Here is a summary of our [PERIOD — e.g., Q3] financial results:

[PASTE OR DESCRIBE YOUR KEY NUMBERS — e.g., revenue up 12% YoY, gross margin down 3 points, operating expenses up 18%, net income flat. Include any context you know about what drove each movement.]

Write a [LENGTH — e.g., two-page] [AUDIENCE — e.g., CFO narrative for the board / investor update / management accounts commentary] that:
- Explains what drove each of these movements
- States what it means for [NEXT PERIOD — e.g., Q4 / the full year]
- Identifies [NUMBER — e.g., three] decisions the audience should be focused on given these results

Tone: direct, confident, assumes [AUDIENCE FINANCIAL LITERACY — e.g., full financial literacy / general business literacy]. No filler language.
```

---

**How to use this:** You are providing the numbers — the model is drafting the prose around them. Read every sentence before it goes to any stakeholder. The risk is not wrong numbers — it is plausible-sounding explanations that don't reflect the actual business context. Your expert review is non-negotiable.

**Extend it for:** Variance analysis write-ups (describe what moved and why; ask for a clearly structured explanation by line item), investor update emails (provide key metrics and messages; ask for a concise investor update), earnings call prep (describe the results and likely analyst concerns; ask for anticipated questions and suggested talking points).
