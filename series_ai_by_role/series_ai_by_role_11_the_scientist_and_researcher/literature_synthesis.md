# Literature Synthesis

Use this to produce a structured synthesis of a set of paper abstracts for a literature review section. A summary that would take a full day takes an hour. You validate it, apply domain expertise, and extend it with the judgment and novelty the model doesn't have.

---

```
Here are abstracts from [NUMBER] papers on [RESEARCH TOPIC]: [PASTE ABSTRACTS, NUMBERED 1 THROUGH N].

From these, give me:
1. A structured synthesis of the key findings grouped by theme — not by paper.
2. Where the findings agree and where they conflict, with the specific papers cited by number.
3. Methodological approaches used across the papers and their key limitations.
4. Questions that remain unanswered or underexplored based on this body of work.

Context: I'm writing [WHAT YOU'RE WRITING — e.g., the literature review section of a journal article / a grant proposal background section / a systematic review introduction].

Tone: [ACADEMIC AND TECHNICAL / ACCESSIBLE FOR A GENERAL SCIENTIFIC AUDIENCE / APPROPRIATE FOR A LAY SUMMARY].
```

---

**How to use this:** Treat the output as a structured draft, not a finished section. Validate every claim against the actual abstracts. Extend the synthesis with papers not in this set, with your own domain knowledge, and with judgments about what the gaps mean for your specific research question.

**Critical rule:** Any citations or paper references the model produces must be independently verified before appearing in a document. Frontier models hallucinate citations — plausible author names, titles, and journal references that don't exist. This is non-negotiable in academic work.

**Extend it for:** Grant proposal drafts (describe your research question, methodology, and significance; ask for a specific aims page or full proposal section), methods section drafts (describe your procedure step by step; ask for a formatted methods section), lay summary (paste your abstract and key findings; ask for a plain-language version for a public audience at a defined reading level).
