# Contract First Draft

Use this to generate a first-pass draft of a standard agreement. The expertise goes into reviewing, identifying the clauses that don't fit the specific parties, and advising on the provisions that matter for this deal — not writing from a blank page.

**Before using:** Confirm your firm's policy on use of public AI tools. Never input actual client names, identifying details, or privileged information. Work with anonymised parameters and apply specific details manually after generation, in an approved environment.

---

```
Draft a [AGREEMENT TYPE — e.g., non-disclosure agreement / service agreement / software licence] between [PARTY A DESCRIPTION — e.g., a UK-based technology startup as the disclosing party] and [PARTY B DESCRIPTION — e.g., a potential enterprise client as the receiving party].

[MUTUAL OR ONE-WAY — e.g., Mutual obligations / One-way obligations in favour of Party A].

Term: [DURATION — e.g., two years from the date of signing].

Covers: [LIST WHAT THE AGREEMENT PROTECTS OR GOVERNS — e.g., product roadmap, pricing, technical architecture, customer data].

Include: [ANY STANDARD CLAUSES TO INCLUDE — e.g., standard carve-outs for publicly known information, prior knowledge, and independently developed materials].

Governing law: [JURISDICTION — e.g., England and Wales].

Flag any clauses where you would typically recommend jurisdiction-specific review or where the drafting involves meaningful optionality.
```

---

**How to use this:** Treat every output as a junior associate's first draft — useful, well-structured, requiring your expert review before it goes anywhere. The model fills the page. You validate the substance. Check every clause against the specific parties and deal context; flag anything that doesn't fit before it is reviewed by anyone else.

**Verify independently:** Frontier models can produce legally plausible but jurisdiction-incorrect clauses, cite cases that don't exist, and miss recent legislative changes. These are real risks — your expert review is what makes the output usable.

**Extend it for:** Client-friendly explanations of complex provisions (paste a clause and ask for a plain-English explanation appropriate for a non-lawyer client), clause comparison (paste two versions of the same clause and ask for a structured comparison of what changed and the implications), brief outlines (describe the case and the legal argument; ask for a brief structure with section headings and the key points for each).
