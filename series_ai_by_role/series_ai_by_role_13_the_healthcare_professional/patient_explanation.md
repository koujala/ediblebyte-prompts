# Patient Explanation

Use this to draft a plain-English explanation of a diagnosis, procedure, or treatment plan for a patient or family member. This task requires your clinical knowledge about the condition — not patient data. The AI helps you communicate your expertise clearly. No identifiable information is needed.

---

```
I need to explain [DIAGNOSIS, CONDITION, PROCEDURE, OR TREATMENT — described in clinical terms] to a patient [OR FAMILY MEMBER — e.g., parent of a child, adult child of an elderly patient].

The patient is [DESCRIBE RELEVANT CONTEXT WITHOUT IDENTIFIERS — e.g., an adult with no prior medical background, a parent who is anxious and likely to have a lot of questions, a patient who has had this explained before but didn't retain it].

Write a clear, plain-English explanation that:
- Describes what [CONDITION/PROCEDURE] is in terms they can understand
- Explains what will happen and what to expect
- Covers what they should watch for and when to contact us
- Answers the most common questions patients ask about this

Literacy level: [PLAIN — accessible to most adults / SIMPLE — shorter sentences, common words only]. Tone: warm, direct, not dismissive.
```

---

**How to use this:** Read every explanation before it reaches a patient or family member. You are responsible for that patient's care — the explanation must be accurate for their specific situation, which the model cannot know. Adjust for anything that doesn't apply, add anything specific to this case, and ensure the tone matches how you want your team to communicate.

**No patient data needed:** This prompt works without any identifying information. The clinical knowledge is yours — the AI helps structure and communicate it.

**Extend it for:** Pre-appointment information sheets (describe the appointment type and what the patient needs to bring and do beforehand; ask for a clear patient-facing information sheet), protocol summaries for team reference (describe the clinical guideline and ask for a condensed, readable summary for team use).
