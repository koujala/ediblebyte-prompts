# First Draft From Brief

The blank page is the hardest part of writing for most people. AI removes that problem entirely — you can have something to react to in sixty seconds. That draft will not be good enough to send. But reacting is dramatically easier than creating from nothing.

```
I need a structured first draft of [WHAT YOU ARE WRITING — email / memo / post / proposal section].

Audience: [WHO — role, level of familiarity with the topic, what they care about]
Purpose: [WHAT YOU WANT THEM TO KNOW, FEEL, OR DO]
Tone: [PROFESSIONAL / WARM / DIRECT / CONVERSATIONAL — pick one primary]
Length: [WORD COUNT OR SENTENCE COUNT]
Context they need to understand this: [ONE OR TWO SENTENCES OF BACKGROUND]
What NOT to include: [ANY TOPIC, PHRASE, OR CLICHÉ TO AVOID]
Desired response from the reader: [REPLY / APPROVE / ATTEND / NOTHING — JUST KNOW]

Write a first draft. Keep it a draft — do not over-polish. I will rewrite the parts that matter in my own words.
```

## The four-step discipline

1. **Brief AI with everything above** — the more you tell it, the less it guesses.
2. **Read the draft as a critic, not an author.** What is in the wrong order? What is missing that only you would know to include?
3. **Rewrite the parts that matter most in your own words.** The opening line, the key argument, the closing call to action — these are where your voice needs to show up.
4. **Use AI to polish, not replace.** Once the substance is yours, bring AI back for tightening sentences, checking consistency, trimming words.

**The key discipline:** AI drafts. You author. The distinction lives in who does the thinking, not who does the typing.
