# Research Synthesis

Two prompts for the research layer of knowledge work: orient quickly on an unfamiliar topic, then synthesise what you gather into something usable.

---

## Prompt A — Fast orientation on an unfamiliar topic

```
I need to quickly understand [TOPIC] well enough to [SPECIFIC OUTCOME — e.g. "hold a 15-minute conversation with a domain expert", "decide whether we should investigate further", "write a short brief for my team"].

I have approximately [TIME BUDGET, e.g. 20 minutes].

Give me:
1. The core concept in plain language — no jargon, no hedging.
2. The two or three things that are genuinely contested, uncertain, or evolving in this space right now.
3. The three questions I should be asking before I form a view — the ones that would reveal whether I'm oversimplifying.
4. One thing a newcomer is likely to get subtly wrong, even after reading a decent overview.

Keep it tight. Skip the history lesson unless it's genuinely necessary to understand the present.
```

---

## Prompt B — Synthesise scattered notes into a narrative

Use this once you've gathered raw material: notes, quotes, sources, data points.

```
Here are my notes and sources on [TOPIC]:

[PASTE EVERYTHING — bullet points, quotes, links, half-formed thoughts]

Synthesise this into a coherent narrative for [AUDIENCE — describe them: their role, what they already know, how much time they have].

The structure I want:
1. **Key finding** — the single most important thing the audience needs to take away. Lead with this.
2. **Supporting evidence** — the 2–4 strongest points from my notes that back the finding. Flag anything where the evidence is thin.
3. **Most significant uncertainty** — what we still don't know, or what reasonable people could disagree on based on what I've gathered.
4. **Recommendation** — a clear, specific call on what the audience should do or decide next.

Keep the audience's constraints in mind — they have limited time and no prior knowledge. Write it for a reader who will make a decision based on this, not one who is here to be entertained.
```

**Tip:** For anything that will carry your name in a professional setting, verify any specific fact before sending. Fluent synthesis is still synthesis — accuracy is your responsibility.
