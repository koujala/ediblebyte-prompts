# Stakeholder Update

The single most useful shift in stakeholder communication is leading with what matters most to *them* — not what is most recent for you. This prompt builds that shift into the output by design.

```
I need to send an update to [STAKEHOLDER OR GROUP] about [PROJECT / SITUATION / DECISION].

About the audience:
- Their primary concern regarding this: [X]
- Their secondary concern: [Y, if relevant]
- Their current awareness: [INFORMED / PARTIALLY INFORMED / NOT YET AWARE of relevant context]
- Their relationship to me / my team: [BRIEFLY]

Content:
- The key thing they need to know: [Y]
- Supporting context they will need in order to understand that: [MINIMAL — only what's actually necessary]
- What I need from them, if anything: [ACTION / DECISION / APPROVAL — or "nothing, just awareness"]
- Anything I should acknowledge up front: [e.g. a missed deadline, a prior concern they raised, a recent event]

Format and constraints:
- Channel: [EMAIL / SLACK MESSAGE / BRIEFING NOTE]
- Maximum length: [WORD COUNT OR LINE COUNT]
- Tone: [FORMAL / PROFESSIONAL-WARM / DIRECT-AND-BRIEF]

Write the update. Lead with what matters most to them, not what is most recent for me. Do not bury the ask. Do not pad. Do not use "I hope this finds you well".
```

**Tip:** Read the output once through the stakeholder's eyes. If the first sentence wouldn't make them lean in, move whatever *would* up to the top.
