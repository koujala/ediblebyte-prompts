# Diagnose Learning Readiness — What Needs to Be True Before This Can Land

Use this prompt before committing to learning a concept or skill — to identify what foundational understanding is missing, and whether the next step is study or lived experience.

---

```
I want to learn [CONCEPT/SKILL — be specific].

Here's where I currently am: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION OF YOUR EXISTING KNOWLEDGE AND RELEVANT EXPERIENCE].

What do I need to already understand — or already have experienced — for this to actually land? What's the foundational thing I might be skipping? And is there something I need to live through rather than study before this can become genuinely usable?
```

---

**How to use:**

This prompt works against the instinct to optimise for learning speed. The article's insight is that sequence matters more than content — a concept introduced before the foundational experience exists cannot be absorbed regardless of how clearly it is explained.

The three questions the response should address:
1. **Prerequisite knowledge:** what conceptual gaps would make the new thing confusing or abstract rather than immediately usable
2. **Prerequisite experience:** what situations or challenges you need to have encountered for the concept to feel real rather than theoretical
3. **The honest answer:** whether the blocker is a book, a course, or something you can only get by doing

The last answer is the most valuable and the hardest to hear. Some things cannot be accelerated. Knowing that before you invest time in the wrong preparation saves both the time and the frustration of learning something that cannot yet stick.

**Use this for advising others too:** before giving a recommendation, ask whether the person you are advising is ready to receive it — and what would need to be true first.
