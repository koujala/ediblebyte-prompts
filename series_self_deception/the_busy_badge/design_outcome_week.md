# Design an Outcome-Defined Week

Use this prompt to break the busyness identity and rebuild your week structure around what moves rather than what fills. The article's prescription: one concrete outcome per week, written before the week starts — everything else is support.

---

## Prompt

```
I have a tendency to fill my time with activity and measure my value by how busy I am. I want to change this.

Help me design a week structure where I commit to three specific outcomes rather than a full calendar — where success is defined by what moved, not what I attended.

My current role and responsibilities: [DESCRIBE YOUR JOB AND MAIN ACCOUNTABILITIES].
My most important goals right now: [LIST 2-3 THINGS THAT ACTUALLY MATTER].

What does that outcome-defined structure look like? What do I have to stop doing or protect against to make room for it?
```

---

**How to use:** Be honest about your actual role and goals. The more specific you are, the more concrete the structure will be. Vague inputs produce vague schedules.

**What to do with the output:** Treat the three outcomes as commitments, not intentions. At the end of the week, check whether they moved. The article's test: if the answer is unclear, busyness is still the job.
